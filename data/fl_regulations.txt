468.8311 — Definitions
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

As used in this part, the term:

  (1) "Department" means the Department of Business and Professional Regulation.

  (2) "Home" means a single-family dwelling, duplex, triplex, quadruplex,
  condominium unit, or cooperative unit; but not the common areas of a
  condominium or cooperative.

  (3) "Home inspector" means any person who provides home inspection services
  for compensation or other consideration.

  (4) "Home inspection services" means a limited visual examination of the
  following readily accessible installed systems and components of a home:
  the structure, electrical system, HVAC system, roof covering, plumbing
  system, interior components, exterior components, and site conditions
  affecting the structure, for the purpose of providing a professional
  opinion on the condition of the home and delivering a written report to
  the client.

  (5) "Inspector of record" means the licensed home inspector who performs
  the inspection and who signs and is responsible for the written report
  delivered to the client.

  (6) "Client" means the person or entity who contracts for and receives the
  home inspection report.

History: s. 2, ch. 2007-235; s. 1, ch. 2010-106; s. 1, ch. 2010-176.

468.8312 — Home inspection services licensing program; purpose
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  The purpose of this part is to establish minimum competency standards and
  ethical guidelines for licensed home inspectors operating in the State of
  Florida. The Legislature finds that home inspections serve the public
  interest by providing objective information about the condition of
  residential property to buyers, sellers, and insurers. It is the intent
  of the Legislature that all home inspectors licensed under this part perform
  services in a manner consistent with recognized standards of practice and
  deliver written reports that are clear, accurate, and useful to the client.

History: s. 2, ch. 2007-235.

468.8313 — Examinations
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  (1) A person desiring to be licensed as a home inspector must apply to the
  department after he or she satisfies the examination requirements of this part.

  (2) An applicant may practice as a home inspector in Florida upon satisfying
  the department that the applicant:
    (a) Has passed a written examination approved by the department testing
        competency in home inspection principles and practice;
    (b) Demonstrates good moral character as defined in subsection (5); and
    (c) Has completed at least 120 hours of study in a curriculum approved
        by the department covering: structure and foundation, electrical systems,
        heating and cooling systems, roof coverings, plumbing systems, interior
        components, exterior components, and site conditions affecting the structure.

  (3) The department shall review and approve courses of study in home inspection
  that meet the requirements of subsection (2)(c).

  (4) The department may review and approve examinations by nationally recognized
  entities that offer programs or set standards ensuring competency as a home
  inspector.

  (5)(a) Good moral character means a personal history of honesty, fairness,
  and respect for the rights of others and for the laws of this state and nation.

  (5)(b) The department may deny certification for lack of good moral character
  only if there is a substantial connection between the lack of good moral
  character and the professional responsibilities of a licensed home inspector,
  and the finding is supported by clear and convincing evidence.

  (5)(c) Any applicant denied certification based on lack of good moral character
  shall be furnished written findings of fact and conclusions of law, the
  evidence relied upon, and notice of the right to a hearing.

  (6) Applicants must submit electronic fingerprints to the Florida Department
  of Law Enforcement and the Federal Bureau of Investigation for criminal history
  checks; costs shall be borne by the applicant.

  (7) The department may adopt rules to implement this section.

History: s. 2, ch. 2007-235; s. 2, ch. 2010-106; s. 2, ch. 2010-176.

468.8314 — Licensure
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  (1) The department shall license any applicant who the department certifies
  is qualified to practice home inspection services.

  (2) The department shall certify an applicant who:
    (a) Satisfies the requirements of s. 468.8313;
    (b) Passes the examination required under s. 468.8313;
    (c) Has not violated any provision of s. 468.8319 or chapter 455; and
    (d) Has paid the applicable fees.

  (3) The department shall certify an applicant for licensure by endorsement who:
    (a) Demonstrates good moral character as required under s. 468.8313(5);
    (b) Maintains the insurance required under s. 468.8322; and
    (c) Either:
      1. Holds a valid home inspector license in another state or territory of
         the United States where the requirements for licensure are substantially
         similar to the requirements of this part; or
      2. Held a valid home inspector license in another state or territory of
         the United States for at least 10 years before the date of application,
         whether currently active or within 2 years of becoming inactive.

  (4) The department shall not issue a license by endorsement to any applicant
  who is under investigation in another state for any act that would constitute
  a violation of this part or chapter 455 until such time as the investigation
  is complete and all disciplinary proceedings have been terminated.

  (5) Each licensed home inspector is the inspector of record for any report
  he or she signs. The inspector of record bears full professional responsibility
  for the content, accuracy, and regulatory compliance of the inspection report
  delivered to the client. AI-generated or computer-assisted narratives do not
  transfer or diminish this responsibility. Each AI-generated report requires
  review and approval by the licensed inspector of record per Florida Statute
  468.8314 before delivery to a client.

History: s. 2, ch. 2007-235; s. 53, ch. 2010-106; s. 22, ch. 2020-160.

468.8315 — Fees
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  The department shall establish fees for licensure by rule. Fees shall be
  sufficient to cover the cost of administering the licensing program, not
  to exceed:
  (a) Application fee: $100
  (b) Initial license fee: $150
  (c) Biennial renewal fee: $200
  (d) Delinquent renewal fee: $50 in addition to the renewal fee

History: s. 2, ch. 2007-235; s. 4, ch. 2010-106.

468.8316 — License renewal
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  (1) The department shall renew a license upon receipt of the renewal
  application, applicable fee, and proof of 14 hours of continuing education
  completed during the preceding biennium.

  (2) Continuing education for renewal must include at minimum:
    (a) 3 hours on Florida Statute Chapter 468 Part XV and applicable rules;
    (b) 3 hours on Florida Building Code updates relevant to residential
        construction; and
    (c) 8 hours of elective topics approved by the department including
        systems inspection techniques, report writing, or relevant standards.

  (3) The department shall adopt rules specifying continuing education
  provider approval, course content requirements, and reporting procedures.

History: s. 2, ch. 2007-235; s. 5, ch. 2010-106; s. 3, ch. 2010-176.

468.8318 — Scope of practice; limitations
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  (1) A home inspection performed under this part is a limited, non-invasive,
  visual examination of the readily accessible installed systems and components
  of a home as defined in s. 468.8311(4).

  (2) A home inspection does NOT include:
    (a) Destructive testing, dismantling, or moving personal property;
    (b) Operating systems or components that are unsafe, do not respond to
        normal operating controls, or are shut down;
    (c) Inspecting or describing inaccessible areas;
    (d) Determining the cause of a deficiency beyond what is visually apparent;
    (e) Providing cost estimates for repairs unless separately contracted;
    (f) Conducting engineering analysis or structural calculations;
    (g) Inspecting for wood-destroying organisms (requires FL Statute 482
        licensed operator);
    (h) Environmental testing including mold sampling, asbestos, lead paint,
        or radon (requires separate licensure under FL Statute 468 Part XVI
        and applicable federal regulations);
    (i) Inspecting wells, septic systems, or private sewage disposal systems
        (requires FL Statute 381 licensed inspector);
    (j) Determining sinkhole activity (requires licensed geotechnical engineer).

  (3) An inspector shall not repair, replace, or otherwise perform corrective
  work on any component of a property that the inspector has inspected for
  compensation within the preceding 12 months, except through a home warranty
  company that employs or is affiliated with the inspector.

History: s. 2, ch. 2007-235; s. 6, ch. 2010-106; s. 4, ch. 2010-176.

468.8319 — Minimum inspection requirements (systems and components)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  A licensed home inspector must inspect all of the following systems and
  components when present and accessible at the time of the inspection:

  (a) Structural system — including foundation type and material, grading and
      drainage conditions, roof covering material and condition, roof structure
      and attic framing when accessible, interior and exterior load-bearing walls,
      columns or piers, floors and floor framing, ceilings, and all visible
      structural components. Inspector must report on any evidence of settling,
      cracking, water intrusion, or deterioration of structural elements.

  (b) Electrical system — including the service entrance conductors and
      equipment, main service panel(s) and sub-panels, overcurrent protection
      devices (breakers and fuses), branch circuit wiring visible or accessible,
      connected devices and fixtures including outlets, switches, and light
      fixtures, smoke detectors and carbon monoxide detectors where required,
      and grounding and bonding connections. Inspector must identify panel
      manufacturer and flag known defective panel types including but not limited
      to Federal Pacific Electric (Stab-Lok), Zinsco/Sylvania, and pushmatic
      panels as high-risk. Inspector must identify aluminum branch circuit wiring.
      Inspector must verify GFCI protection in required locations per the adopted
      edition of the National Electrical Code.

  (c) HVAC system — including all heating and cooling equipment present,
      distribution systems (ductwork, registers, and grilles), thermostats and
      controls, energy sources, and ventilation components. Inspector must report
      the approximate age and operational status of equipment, identify refrigerant
      type when determinable, and flag ductwork deficiencies including disconnections,
      collapses, or inadequate insulation in unconditioned spaces.

  (d) Plumbing system — including interior water supply and distribution piping
      (material, condition, and any visible defects), all drain, waste, and vent
      piping visible and accessible, water heating equipment (type, age, capacity,
      TPR valve condition), all fixtures and faucets, and the water supply source
      and waste disposal method. Inspector must identify polybutylene supply piping
      (a known defective material — Quest, Shell Oil, DuPont manufacture, gray
      or blue-gray color, installed approximately 1978–1995) and report it as a
      critical deficiency. Inspector must identify galvanized drain or supply lines
      showing significant corrosion and report expected remaining service life.

  (e) Roofing system — including the roof covering material and condition,
      flashings at penetrations, valleys, and intersections, roof drainage
      components (gutters and downspouts when present), any visible skylights,
      and accessible roof ventilation. For 4-Point inspections, the inspector
      must estimate the remaining useful life of the roof covering.

  (f) Appliances — including all installed and permanently connected appliances:
      dishwashers, ranges, cooktops, ovens, garbage disposals, microwave ovens
      built into cabinetry, bathroom exhaust fans, and garage door openers.
      Inspector shall operate each appliance using normal operating controls
      and report on observable functional status.

  (g) Exterior — including exterior wall cladding, flashing, and trim;
      all exterior doors and accessible windows; decks, balconies, and stoops;
      attached and adjacent garages; driveways and walkways for trip hazards;
      grading slope away from foundation (minimum 6 inches in first 10 feet
      per FBC R401.3); and visible foundation elements.

  (h) Interior — including interior walls, ceilings, and floors for evidence
      of water intrusion, structural movement, or deterioration; interior doors
      and windows; stairways and railings; fireplaces and fuel-burning appliances
      visible and accessible; and attic access and visible insulation.

History: s. 2, ch. 2007-235; s. 7, ch. 2010-106; s. 5, ch. 2010-176; s. 11, ch. 2011-222.

468.8319(b) — Electrical System — Detailed Inspection Requirements
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Under s. 468.8319(b), the inspector must examine and report on:

  Service Entrance and Main Panel:
    - Service amperage (60A, 100A, 150A, 200A, 400A)
    - Panel manufacturer, model, and approximate age
    - Federal Pacific Electric (Stab-Lok) panels: must be flagged as critical
      safety deficiency — documented failure to trip under overcurrent conditions
      creates fire and electrocution risk; non-insurable with most FL carriers
    - Zinsco / Sylvania panels: must be flagged as critical — breaker-to-bus bar
      connection failures create fire risk; non-insurable with most FL carriers
    - Double-tapped breakers: two conductors on a single breaker not designed
      for dual connections violates NEC 210.4(B) and must be corrected
    - Missing breaker knockouts, open panel openings, missing cover plates
    - Evidence of heat damage, arcing, or corrosion inside panel

  Branch Circuit Wiring:
    - Aluminum branch circuit wiring: post-1965 aluminum wiring in 15A and 20A
      circuits creates fire risk at connection points due to expansion/contraction.
      Must identify and recommend COPALUM crimp connectors (approved by CPSC) or
      AlumiConn devices per NEC 310.14 and FL requirements. Non-insurable without
      remediation with most FL carriers.
    - Knob-and-tube wiring (pre-1950): identify and note as non-insurable by
      most FL carriers; cannot be covered with insulation per NEC 394.12
    - Cloth-wrapped wiring (NM cable with fabric outer jacket): identify and note
      age-related brittleness risk
    - Unprotected or exposed wiring in living areas
    - Open junction boxes without covers
    - Evidence of DIY wiring (improper splices, missing wire connectors)

  GFCI Protection Requirements (NEC 210.8 as adopted by FBC):
    - Required within 6 feet of all sink locations including kitchen, bathrooms,
      wet bars, and laundry areas
    - Required in all garage locations including attached and detached garages
    - Required in unfinished basements and crawlspaces
    - Required at all exterior outlets
    - Required at all pool, spa, and fountain equipment
    - Required at all boathouse wiring
    - Required at all rooftop outlets
    - Absence of GFCI protection in any required location must be reported as
      a safety deficiency requiring correction

  AFCI Protection Requirements (NEC 210.12 as adopted by FBC):
    - Arc-fault circuit-interrupter protection required in all habitable rooms
      including bedrooms, living rooms, dining rooms, family rooms, and dens
      per FBC E3902 and NEC 210.12
    - Combination-type AFCI breakers required in new construction and major
      renovations; absence in older homes should be noted as a safety upgrade
      recommendation

  Smoke and Carbon Monoxide Detectors:
    - Smoke detectors required in each sleeping room, outside each sleeping
      area, and on each level including basements per NFPA 72 and FBC R314
    - Carbon monoxide alarms required in dwelling units with fuel-burning
      appliances or attached garages per FBC R315
    - Inspector must test each device using test button; report non-responsive
      units as deficiencies requiring replacement

History: s. 2, ch. 2007-235 (detail regulation); NEC 2020 adopted per s. 553.73, F.S.

468.8319(d) — Plumbing System — Detailed Inspection Requirements
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Under s. 468.8319(d), the inspector must examine and report on:

  Supply Piping:
    - Identify material: copper (type K, L, or M), CPVC (cream or off-white
      chlorinated PVC), PVC (white, not suitable for hot water), PEX (flexible,
      red/blue/white), galvanized steel (gray metallic, threaded connections),
      or polybutylene (gray/blue-gray, Quest or Shell Oil brand markings)
    - Polybutylene (PB) is a critical deficiency:
      * Installed approximately 1978–1995
      * Quest, Shell Oil, and DuPont manufactured versions
      * Gray or blue-gray color; may have copper or plastic compression fittings
      * Class action settlement (Cox v. Shell Oil) exhausted in early 2000s
      * Known failure mode: acetal plastic fittings degrade under chlorine exposure,
        leading to splitting, pinhole leaks, and catastrophic failure
      * Non-insurable with Citizens and most major FL carriers without replacement
      * Inspector must report all visible PB piping as critical requiring replacement
    - Galvanized steel pipe: iron pipe with zinc coating; acceptable for cold water
      only; pipe older than 30–40 years must be flagged for internal corrosion,
      reduced flow, and leakage risk
    - All active leaks at supply connections must be reported as deficiencies
      requiring immediate repair

  Drain, Waste, and Vent (DWV) Systems:
    - Identify material: cast iron, ABS (black plastic), PVC (white/gray plastic),
      or galvanized (older homes)
    - Cast iron DWV with significant pitting, holes, or joint failures must be
      reported; serviceable life typically 50–75 years
    - Verify proper venting: sewage odors in the home may indicate failed vent
      pipes; inspector must note and recommend licensed plumber evaluation
    - Drain slope: minimum 1/4 inch per foot for 3-inch and smaller drains
      per FBC Plumbing Code P3005.3

  Water Heater:
    - Record age (from serial number date codes), fuel type (electric, gas,
      heat pump), capacity in gallons, and visible condition
    - Water heaters 15 years or older: flag as at or beyond expected serviceable
      life (typical serviceable life 10–15 years for electric, 8–12 years for gas);
      recommend budgeting for replacement
    - Temperature-Pressure Relief (TPR) valve:
      * Must be present and accessible
      * Discharge pipe must terminate within 6 inches of floor or discharge to
        an appropriate drain; upward-facing discharge is a safety hazard
        (scalding risk when TPR activates)
      * Discharge pipe may not be threaded, capped, or reduced in size below
        the TPR valve outlet
      * Per FBC Plumbing Code P2803 and ANSI Z21.22
    - Water heaters may not be installed in sleeping rooms or closets that
      open to sleeping rooms per FBC R2004.1
    - Gas water heater combustion air supply must be verified per FBC M1307

  Fixtures and Faucets:
    - All accessible fixtures (toilets, sinks, tubs, showers) must be operated
      and tested for proper function, leaks, and adequate water pressure
    - Toilet bases must be checked for rocking (indicating failed wax ring)
    - Shower pans must be noted if visible damage or grout failure suggests
      potential water intrusion to subfloor

History: s. 2, ch. 2007-235; FBC Plumbing Code, 7th Ed. (2023); ANSI Z21.22.

468.8319(a) — Structural System — Detailed Inspection Requirements
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Under s. 468.8319(a), the inspector must examine and report on:

  Foundation:
    - Identify type: slab-on-grade, continuous perimeter footing, crawlspace
      with piers, or basement (rare in FL)
    - Report visible cracking in foundation elements: hairline cracks (minor,
      typical shrinkage), horizontal cracks in masonry (potentially serious,
      may indicate lateral pressure), stair-step diagonal cracking in concrete
      block (may indicate differential settlement or sinkhole activity)
    - Florida karst geology creates sinkhole risk; stair-step diagonal cracking
      at corners, floor slopes exceeding 1 inch over 10 feet, circular depressions
      in yard adjacent to structure, and door/window binding may indicate
      differential settlement — inspector must refer to licensed geotechnical
      engineer; inspector may not determine sinkhole activity
    - Significant settlement indicators require immediate specialist referral

  Roof Structure and Attic:
    - Access and visually examine attic when accessible
    - Inspect roof framing members (rafters, trusses, ridge board) for damage,
      insect infestation, water staining, or inadequate repairs
    - Report evidence of past or active water intrusion (staining, mold, rot)
    - Verify ventilation: soffit-to-ridge ventilation recommended; inadequate
      ventilation accelerates shingle aging and causes moisture accumulation
    - Identify roof sheathing type: OSB or plywood; note any delamination,
      buckling, or deterioration
    - For wind mitigation: identify nail type and spacing (8d @ 6/12 maximum
      credit), sheathing thickness, and presence of secondary water resistance

  Exterior Walls:
    - Identify cladding type: concrete block (CBS — concrete block structure,
      dominant in Florida), stucco-over-frame, EIFS/synthetic stucco, vinyl
      or aluminum siding, wood siding, or fiber cement
    - Stucco inspection: look for cracking patterns indicating water intrusion
      (map cracking may be cosmetic; horizontal cracking at floors or around
      openings may indicate structural movement or moisture intrusion)
    - EIFS/synthetic stucco: heightened moisture risk; note for further evaluation
    - Wood components: check for wood rot at all ground-contact areas, window
      and door frames, sills, fascia, soffit, and trim

  Floor System:
    - Assess floor levelness and rigidity; soft spots may indicate subfloor
      deterioration, fungal damage, or structural concerns
    - Crawlspace access: inspect for moisture vapor, standing water, inadequate
      clearances (minimum 18 inches to bottom of floor framing per FBC R408),
      wood contact with soil, and vapor retarder condition

History: s. 2, ch. 2007-235; FBC R401–R408; s. 553.73, F.S. (FBC adoption).

468.8319(c) — HVAC System — Detailed Inspection Requirements
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Under s. 468.8319(c), the inspector must examine and report on:

  Heating and Cooling Equipment:
    - Record equipment type (central split-system AC, heat pump, mini-split,
      package unit, window unit, electric baseboard, natural gas furnace),
      manufacturer, model number, and approximate age from serial number
    - HVAC equipment older than 15 years: flag as at or approaching end of
      expected serviceable life (central AC 12–15 years, heat pump 15 years,
      gas furnace 15–20 years); recommend budgeting for replacement
    - Record refrigerant type: R-22 (Freon) systems phased out under EPA
      Section 608 regulations; R-22 is no longer manufactured and available
      only as reclaimed refrigerant at significantly higher cost; flag R-22
      systems as reaching end of supportable service life
    - Measure and report temperature differential across evaporator coil
      (acceptable range typically 14–22°F); values outside this range suggest
      low refrigerant, dirty coil, or airflow restriction
    - Inspect accessible evaporator coil for ice formation, evidence of
      refrigerant oil (indicating leak), or excessive biological growth on drain pan
    - Inspect condenser unit: clear minimum 24-inch clearance from vegetation,
      fins for mechanical damage, and electrical disconnect condition

  Ductwork and Distribution:
    - Identify duct material: sheet metal (most durable), flex duct (commonly
      used in FL), ductboard (foil-faced fiberglass board), or older cloth-wrapped
      or asbestos-wrapped (hazardous if deteriorating — refer for testing)
    - All ductwork in unconditioned attic spaces must be insulated to minimum
      R-6 per FBC Energy Code (ASHRAE 90.1 as adopted)
    - Disconnected duct joints in attic: must be flagged as critical — conditioned
      air losses of 20–40% significantly impact efficiency and may cause moisture
      problems; pressurization of unconditioned attic space can drive moisture
      into structure
    - Crushed or kinked flex duct must be reported; reduces airflow and
      efficiency; may cause condensation
    - Supply and return register locations should be assessed for balanced
      distribution; absent return air in bedrooms with closed doors may cause
      pressure imbalance

  Ventilation:
    - Bathroom exhaust fans must be ducted to exterior; discharge into attic
      is a code violation and moisture deficiency (FBC M1507.2 and
      FL Residential Energy Code); must be corrected
    - Kitchen exhaust fans (range hoods): if ducted, must terminate at exterior;
      recirculating (ductless) type must be noted
    - Clothes dryer vents must terminate at building exterior; maximum developed
      length 25 feet (35 feet with manufacturer approval); per FBC M1502
    - Dryer vent through wall to attic or crawlspace is a code violation and
      fire risk

  Thermostat and Controls:
    - Operate thermostat through both heating and cooling cycles
    - Note non-responsive thermostat or equipment that fails to activate

History: s. 2, ch. 2007-235; FBC Mechanical Code; ASHRAE 62.2-2019.

468.8319(e) — Roof System — Detailed Inspection Requirements
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Under s. 468.8319(e), the inspector must examine and report on:

  Roof Covering Material:
    - Identify material: asphalt composition shingles, architectural/dimensional
      shingles, concrete tile, clay tile, metal (standing seam, exposed fastener,
      5-V crimp), built-up (tar and gravel), modified bitumen, or single-ply
      membrane (TPO, EPDM)
    - Document approximate age from seller disclosure, permit records, or
      manufacturer code; if unknown, estimate based on condition
    - Condition rating for 4-Point inspection: Excellent / Good / Fair / Poor
    - Remaining useful life estimate (in years) required for 4-Point insurance forms

  Asphalt Shingles — Deficiency Indicators:
    - Granule loss: excessive granule loss exposing fiberglass mat indicates
      end of serviceable life (normal life 20–25 years for 3-tab, 25–30 years
      for architectural); granule loss exceeding 25% of surface area is significant
    - Curling, cupping, or clawing of shingles: thermal cycling deformation
      indicating age and potential for wind uplift damage
    - Missing shingles: any missing units must be noted; creates immediate
      water intrusion risk
    - Improper or mismatched repairs: evidence of prior patch repairs using
      different shingle type or color; note for appearance and potential sealing issues
    - Ridge and hip cap condition: these areas typically fail first; assess
      for integrity

  Flashings:
    - Inspect all roof penetration flashings: chimneys, plumbing vents, skylights,
      HVAC equipment curbs
    - Step flashing at wall-to-roof intersections; counter-flashing at masonry
    - Valley flashings: open metal valley (most durable in FL) vs. closed cut
    - Absence of, or improperly installed, flashings at any penetration is a
      water intrusion risk requiring correction

  Evidence of Water Intrusion:
    - In attic: water staining on decking (brown rings), deteriorated or
      discolored insulation, mold growth on framing, rot in wood members
    - Active leaks must be clearly noted as immediate deficiencies
    - Prior staining without active leak: report as evidence of prior leakage;
      recommend correction of source before future events

  Gutters and Downspouts:
    - Condition and proper slope toward downspouts
    - Downspouts must discharge away from foundation (minimum 4 feet) or
      connect to underground drainage
    - Missing or damaged sections noted

History: s. 2, ch. 2007-235; Citizens 4-Point Form HO-800; FBC R905.

468.8320 — Standards of practice; minimum inspection procedures
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  (1) A home inspector performing services under this part shall conduct the
  inspection in accordance with the standards of practice adopted by rule by
  the department. At minimum, the inspector shall:

    (a) Inspect all systems listed in s. 468.8319 that are present and accessible
        at the time of the inspection, using normal operating controls;
    (b) Report any system or component that is significantly deficient,
        unsafe, near the end of its service life, or not functioning as intended;
    (c) Report which systems or components were present but not inspected
        and state the reason they were not inspected;
    (d) Conduct the inspection in a manner that does not damage property or
        create safety hazards for the inspector, occupants, or bystanders;
    (e) Use appropriate equipment including electrical testers, moisture meters
        (when moisture intrusion is suspected), and binoculars for elevated or
        distant roof observation.

  (2) An inspection is not required to include:
    (a) Items listed in s. 468.8318(2);
    (b) Systems or components that cannot be safely operated, are disconnected,
        or are inaccessible;
    (c) Disassembly of any component.

  (3) The inspector shall exercise reasonable care in determining system and
  component condition; the inspection is a professional opinion based on
  visible conditions at the time of inspection, not a warranty or guarantee
  of the future performance of any component.

History: s. 2, ch. 2007-235; s. 8, ch. 2010-106.

468.8321 — Disclosures
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Prior to contracting for or commencing a home inspection, a home inspector
  shall provide to the consumer:

  (a) A copy of his or her license to practice home inspection services in
      this state showing the license number and expiration date; and

  (b) A written disclosure that contains:
    1. The scope of the home inspection — specifically which systems will be
       inspected and which systems or components are excluded from the inspection;
    2. Any limitations on the inspection including access restrictions,
       weather conditions, or occupancy that may limit the scope;
    3. A statement that the inspection is a visual examination of accessible
       components and is not a warranty, guarantee, or building code compliance
       determination; and
    4. If the inspector intends to use any automated analysis tools,
       artificial intelligence systems, or computerized report generation,
       a disclosure that such tools assist but do not replace the professional
       judgment of the licensed inspector of record, and that all final
       report content is the responsibility of the inspector of record.

History: s. 2, ch. 2007-235; s. 9, ch. 2010-106.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
468.8322 — Insurance requirement
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  A home inspector shall maintain a commercial general liability insurance
  policy in an amount of not less than $300,000 per occurrence to cover
  claims arising from the performance of home inspection services. Proof of
  insurance must be provided to the department upon request and to the client
  upon request before or at the time of inspection.

History: s. 2, ch. 2007-235.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
468.8323 — Home inspection report requirements
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  (1) Upon completion of each home inspection for compensation, the home
  inspector shall provide a written report prepared for the client.

  (2) The home inspector shall report:

    (a) On those systems and components inspected that, in the professional
        opinion of the inspector, are significantly deficient or are near
        the end of their service lives;

    (b) The reason why a system or component is significantly deficient or
        near the end of its service life, if not self-evident from the
        reported condition;

    (c) Any systems and components that were present at the time of the
        inspection but were not inspected, and the reason they were not
        inspected.

  (3) A home inspector is not required to provide estimates related to the
  cost of repair of an inspected property.

  (4) The written report shall:
    (a) Be written in plain language understandable to a layperson;
    (b) Include the address of the property inspected;
    (c) Include the date and time of the inspection;
    (d) Include the name, license number, and signature of the inspector of record;
    (e) Include photographs of material defects where practical;
    (f) Describe observed conditions — not make legal conclusions regarding
        liability or habitability;
    (g) Be delivered to the client within three (3) business days of the
        inspection unless otherwise agreed in writing.

  (5) If any portion of the written report is generated by an automated system,
  artificial intelligence model, or computer-assisted tool, the report must
  include a disclosure that:
    (a) Identifies which portions are AI-generated or computer-assisted; and
    (b) States that all AI-generated content has been reviewed and approved
        by the licensed inspector of record named in the report.

  The following disclosure language satisfies paragraph (5): "This narrative
  was AI-generated and requires review and approval by the licensed inspector
  of record per Florida Statute 468.8314."

  (6) Severity classification in written reports:
    (a) "Critical" or "Safety Hazard": conditions that pose an immediate risk
        to life or safety, or that would render the property uninsurable under
        standard Florida insurance underwriting guidelines;
    (b) "Major" or "Significant Deficiency": conditions that are significantly
        deficient and require correction but are not immediately life-threatening;
    (c) "Minor" or "Maintenance Item": conditions that require routine maintenance
        or monitoring but do not pose safety risks or significantly impact
        insurability.

History: s. 2, ch. 2007-235; s. 9, ch. 2011-222.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
468.8324 — Disciplinary actions
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  (1) The following acts constitute grounds for disciplinary action under
  chapter 455, F.S.:

    (a) Practicing as a home inspector without a valid license;
    (b) Violating any provision of s. 468.8319 (prohibited acts);
    (c) Making misleading, deceptive, or fraudulent representations in the
        practice of home inspection;
    (d) Failing to perform a home inspection with the care, skill, and
        treatment that a reasonably prudent home inspector would exercise
        under similar circumstances;
    (e) Performing repairs on a property the inspector has recently inspected,
        except as permitted under s. 468.8318(3);
    (f) Failing to maintain required insurance under s. 468.8322;
    (g) Failing to provide required disclosures under s. 468.8321;
    (h) Delegating professional responsibility to an unlicensed person;
    (i) Accepting compensation from undisclosed parties with interest in the
        transaction being inspected;
    (j) Submitting a false inspection report to an insurance company, lender,
        or other party.

  (2) Disciplinary penalties may include:
    (a) Denial, suspension, or revocation of license;
    (b) Administrative fine not exceeding $5,000 per violation;
    (c) Probation with specified conditions;
    (d) Reprimand;
    (e) Requirement to complete additional continuing education;
    (f) Requirement to submit reports to the department for a period of time.

  (3) All disciplinary proceedings shall be conducted pursuant to chapter 120, F.S.
  and chapter 455, F.S.

  (4) An inspector whose license is revoked may not apply for reinstatement
  for five years from the date of revocation.

History: s. 2, ch. 2007-235; s. 10, ch. 2010-106.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
468.8325 — Rulemaking authority
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  The Department of Business and Professional Regulation shall adopt rules
  pursuant to ss. 120.536(1) and 120.54 to administer this part including:
    (a) Standards of practice for home inspectors;
    (b) Application, examination, and renewal procedures and fees;
    (c) Continuing education course approval;
    (d) Disciplinary procedures consistent with chapter 455, F.S.;
    (e) Forms and procedures for home inspection reports.

History: s. 23, ch. 2010-106; s. 15, ch. 2010-176.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
468.8326 — Written report; disclaimer; AI-generated content
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  (1) The written inspection report required under s. 468.8323 constitutes
  the deliverable product of the licensed home inspector's professional
  services and is the sole responsibility of the inspector of record.

  (2) No automated system, software platform, or artificial intelligence
  tool may substitute for the professional judgment of the licensed inspector
  of record. The inspector of record must:
    (a) Review all report content regardless of how generated;
    (b) Verify that the report accurately reflects conditions observed during
        the inspection;
    (c) Correct or reject any AI-generated content that misrepresents observed
        conditions, misattributes regulatory violations, or contains factual errors;
    (d) Sign the final report, certifying that it represents his or her
        professional findings.

  (3) AI disclaimer requirement: Any home inspection report that includes
  content generated by an artificial intelligence model, large language model,
  or automated narrative generation system must include the following or
  substantially similar language in each AI-generated section:
    "This narrative was AI-generated and requires review and approval by
    the licensed inspector of record per Florida Statute 468.8314."

  (4) Delivery requirements:
    (a) Reports shall be delivered to the client within three (3) business days
        of the inspection unless a different period is agreed upon in writing;
    (b) The report may be delivered electronically;
    (c) The inspector shall retain a copy of the report for not less than
        five (5) years.

  (5) Report format requirements:
    (a) Written in plain language a homeowner can understand;
    (b) Free of ambiguous or misleading terminology;
    (c) Contains the property address, inspection date, inspector name,
        and DBPR license number;
    (d) Includes photographs of material defects where practical;
    (e) Identifies which systems were inspected and which were not, with reasons.

History: s. 2, ch. 2007-235; s. 12, ch. 2011-222.

SECTION 2: FLORIDA ADMINISTRATIVE CODE 61-30 — STANDARDS OF PRACTICE
================================================================================

Florida Administrative Code Rule 61-30 establishes the minimum standards of
practice for licensed home inspectors. These rules are promulgated by the
DBPR pursuant to s. 468.8325, F.S., and carry the force of law.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
FAC 61-30.001 — Purpose and scope
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  These rules establish the minimum standards to which a licensed Florida
  home inspector must adhere when performing home inspection services as
  defined in s. 468.8311(4), F.S. Compliance with these standards represents
  the baseline of acceptable professional practice. An inspector may always
  exceed these minimum standards.

  These rules do not prevent an inspector from:
  (a) Expanding the scope of an inspection by mutual written agreement with
      the client;
  (b) Reporting on conditions outside the minimum inspection scope when
      observed in the course of the inspection; or
  (c) Recommending specialist evaluation when conditions suggest a need
      beyond the scope of a home inspection.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
FAC 61-30.002 — General inspection conditions
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  (1) The inspector shall perform the inspection under normal operating
  conditions:
    (a) All systems capable of being operated using normal controls shall be
        operated;
    (b) Systems that are safely off, disconnected, or do not respond to
        normal controls shall be noted as such in the report;
    (c) The inspector shall not operate any system or component that creates
        risk of damage to property or injury to persons.

  (2) Limitations:
    The inspector shall note in the report any limitation on the inspection
    resulting from:
    (a) Inaccessible areas;
    (b) Inoperable systems;
    (c) Weather conditions affecting exterior inspection;
    (d) Occupant's personal property obscuring access;
    (e) Disconnected utilities.

  (3) Ancillary systems outside the scope of this rule:
    The following require separate licensure and referral:
    (a) Wood-destroying organism (WDO) inspections — FL Statute 482;
    (b) Mold assessment and remediation — FL Statute 468 Part XVI;
    (c) Environmental testing (asbestos, lead, radon);
    (d) Well water testing — FL DOH;
    (e) Septic system inspection — FL DOH / FL DEP;
    (f) Geotechnical or engineering analysis.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
FAC 61-30.003 — Structural inspection standards
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  The inspector shall inspect the structural components of the home including:

  (1) Foundation:
    - Inspect all accessible foundation elements
    - Note type: slab-on-grade, spread footing, piers/posts, continuous
    - Report significant cracking, settlement, heave, or deterioration
    - Note any evidence of moisture penetration through foundation

  (2) Framing:
    - Inspect accessible framing members for structural integrity
    - Identify damage from water, insects, fire, or mechanical causes
    - Note inadequate bearing, improper splices, or missing fasteners
    - Report modifications to structural members (notching, boring) that
      appear to violate structural requirements

  (3) Roof structure:
    - Access and inspect attic when an access opening exists and conditions
      permit safe entry
    - Report condition of rafters, trusses, ridge board, and collar ties
    - Note water staining, mold, rot, or insect damage
    - Verify minimum required insulation levels (FBC Energy Code R402)
    - Identify roof deck sheathing type and condition

  (4) Deficiency classification for structural findings:
    - Critical: any condition creating imminent risk of structural failure,
      collapse, or injury
    - Major: significant deficiency requiring correction; may affect
      habitability or insurance eligibility
    - Minor: condition that should be monitored or maintained

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
FAC 61-30.004 — Electrical inspection standards
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  The inspector shall inspect electrical systems including:

  (1) Service entrance and panels:
    - Inspect accessible service entrance conductors, drip loops, and weatherhead
    - Open main panel and sub-panels; report condition of bus bars, breakers,
      wiring connections, and grounding
    - Identify and flag these known defective panel types:
      * Federal Pacific Electric (FPE) Stab-Lok: documented failure-to-trip
        under overcurrent; fire risk; flag as critical deficiency
      * Zinsco/Sylvania: similar failure mode; flag as critical deficiency
      * Pushmatic (ITE/Bulldog): actuator mechanism failures; recommend evaluation
    - Report double-tapped breakers (non-approved multi-wire configurations)
    - Note any evidence of overheating, arcing, or corrosion

  (2) Branch circuits and wiring:
    - Inspect visible wiring in attic, basement, crawlspace, and garage
    - Identify aluminum branch circuit wiring; report as requiring COPALUM or
      AlumiConn remediation
    - Identify knob-and-tube; note insurance and coverage restrictions
    - Verify GFCI protection in all required locations
    - Note absence of AFCI protection in habitable rooms as safety recommendation

  (3) Grounding and bonding:
    - Verify ground rod or grounding electrode system presence
    - Verify bonding of metallic plumbing and gas lines
    - Note absence of grounding conductor on 3-prong outlets (open ground)

  (4) Deficiency language for electrical findings:
    - "Critical" or "Safety Hazard" language required for:
      * FPE Stab-Lok or Zinsco panels (s. 468.8323(6)(a))
      * Aluminum branch circuit wiring without proper remediation
      * Active sparking, burning smell, or visible arcing
      * Wiring with damaged insulation creating exposed conductors
    - "Major" for: double-tapped breakers, missing GFCI where required,
      open junction boxes, amateur wiring repairs
    - "Minor" for: single missing cover plate, minor labeling issue

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
FAC 61-30.005 — HVAC inspection standards
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  The inspector shall inspect heating and cooling systems including:

  (1) Equipment inspection:
    - Operate each HVAC unit through at least one complete heating or
      cooling cycle using normal thermostat controls
    - Verify temperature differential across evaporator coil: acceptable
      range 14–22°F between return and supply air
    - Record estimated age from serial number decode; note if at end of
      expected service life
    - Inspect accessible refrigerant lines for insulation, damage, and
      evidence of oil at fittings (refrigerant leak indicator)
    - Note if refrigerant type is R-22 (phased out; limited service options)

  (2) Ductwork inspection:
    - Access attic and accessible crawlspace to inspect duct condition
    - Note all disconnected, crushed, or collapsed duct sections; classify
      based on severity and impact on conditioned space
    - Verify insulation on attic ductwork (R-6 minimum per FBC Energy Code)
    - Note supply and return balance; single return in large home may indicate
      inadequate air circulation

  (3) Ventilation inspection:
    - Inspect each bathroom exhaust fan: operate and verify discharge to
      exterior (not attic) per FBC M1507; attic discharge is a code violation
    - Inspect dryer vent termination and length
    - Verify combustion air supply for gas appliances

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
FAC 61-30.006 — Plumbing inspection standards
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  The inspector shall inspect plumbing systems including:

  (1) Water supply and distribution:
    - Identify pipe material throughout visible supply system
    - Polybutylene (PB) pipe: mandatory critical deficiency reporting
      * Verify by color (gray/blue-gray), fittings (gray acetal plastic or
        copper compression fittings), and manufacturer markings (Quest,
        Shell Oil, or DuPont QUEST)
      * Active or past leaks at fittings must be noted
      * Even if no active leak, PB must be reported as critical due to
        known failure history and insurance non-eligibility
    - Operate all accessible faucets; check water pressure (acceptable 40-80 psi)
    - Note cross-connections or improper installations

  (2) Drain, waste, and vent:
    - Inspect accessible DWV piping; note material and condition
    - Operate all fixtures; verify adequate drain flow
    - Note sewage odors suggesting vent failure
    - Verify floor drain cover plates in place where accessible

  (3) Water heating:
    - Inspect all water heaters; record age, type, capacity, and condition
    - Verify TPR valve present, operational, and properly discharged
    - Note if unit is at or near end of serviceable life

  (4) Fixtures:
    - Inspect all toilets for rocking base, running, or flushing deficiencies
    - Inspect showers and tubs: note cracked tile, failed grout, and caulking
    - Inspect under sinks for evidence of past or active leaks

SECTION 3: 4-POINT INSPECTION REQUIREMENTS (Citizens Form HO-800)
================================================================================

The 4-Point Inspection is required by Citizens Property Insurance Corporation
and most Florida insurers for homes generally 30 years old or older. The
inspection evaluates only four major systems: roofing, electrical, plumbing,
and HVAC. The current form is Citizens HO-800 Rev. 11/2021.

References: Citizens HO-800 Form; FL Statute 627.062; FL Statute 627.0628.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 3A — ROOF SYSTEM (Citizens HO-800 Section 1)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Inspector Responsibilities:
  The inspector must determine and document all of the following:

  1. Roof covering material:
     - Asphalt shingles (3-tab or architectural/dimensional)
     - Concrete tile
     - Clay tile
     - Metal (standing seam, exposed fastener, or 5-V crimp)
     - Built-up (tar and gravel)
     - Modified bitumen (torch-applied, cold-applied, or self-adhering)
     - Single-ply membrane (TPO, EPDM, PVC)
     - Wood shake or shingles (non-insurable with most FL carriers)

  2. Estimated age of current roof covering (in years):
     - Derived from: prior inspection records, homeowner documentation,
       permit records, material characteristics, or inspector's professional
       estimate
     - Multiple layers of shingles indicate re-roofing history; must note
       number of layers (maximum 2 layers permitted by FBC R905.1.1)

  3. Roof covering condition rating:
     - Excellent: no deficiencies observed; material performing as expected
       for age; no evidence of prior or current leakage
     - Good: minor wear consistent with age; no deficiencies requiring
       immediate attention; some routine maintenance items
     - Fair: evidence of deterioration; significant granule loss or
       mechanical damage; serviceable but approaching end of life
     - Poor: significant deficiencies; active or recent leakage; missing
       materials; structural concerns at roof level

  4. Estimated remaining useful life:
     - Based on material type, age, current condition, and environmental
       exposure; expressed in years
     - Asphalt 3-tab: 15–20 years total; remaining = total – current age
     - Architectural/dimensional shingles: 25–30 years total
     - Concrete or clay tile: 40–50 years total
     - Metal: 40–70 years depending on type
     - Modified bitumen/built-up: 20–30 years

  5. Evidence of leaks or prior leaks:
     - Active leak: water visible entering structure at time of inspection;
       critical deficiency requiring immediate repair
     - Prior leakage: water staining on decking, ceiling, or interior
       framing without current active leak; must be noted and cause identified
     - Inspector must inspect accessible attic for staining, mold, or rot

  6. Specific deficiency triggers (immediate notation required):
     - Active water intrusion through roof covering
     - Missing shingles, tiles, or membrane sections
     - Shingles with granule loss exceeding 25% of total surface area
     - Exposed fiberglass mat or base sheet
     - Lifted, buckled, or curled shingles susceptible to wind uplift
     - Fascia or soffit structural damage
     - Missing, damaged, or improperly installed flashing at any penetration
     - Evidence of prior repairs without permit (mismatched materials, inconsistent
       patterns, visible re-nailing patterns different from original)
     - More than two layers of roofing material (FBC R905.1.1 violation)
     - Flat or low-slope roofs with ponding water (inadequate drainage)
     - Blistering or bubbling of membrane (moisture beneath membrane)
     - Visible sagging or deformation of roof decking or structural members

  Regulatory references for roof findings:
     - FBC R905 — Requirements for Roof Coverings by Type
     - FBC R905.2 — Asphalt Shingle Requirements
     - FBC R905.3 — Clay and Concrete Tile
     - FBC R905.10 — Metal Roof Panels
     - FBC R905.14 — Thermoplastic Single-Ply Roofing
     - FBC R903 — Weather Protection (flashings)
     - Citizens Property Insurance Corporation HO-800 Rev. 11/2021

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 3B — ELECTRICAL SYSTEM (Citizens HO-800 Section 2)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Inspector Responsibilities:
  The inspector must determine and document all of the following:

  1. Service amperage:
     - Determine and report main service amperage
     - 60A or less: may be insufficient for modern load; note as potential
       underservice for contemporary requirements
     - 100A: minimum acceptable for modest single-family home
     - 150A or 200A: standard for most FL single-family homes built after 1980
     - 400A: large homes or homes with substantial electric vehicle or pool loads

  2. Panel type and manufacturer:
     - Identify manufacturer and model number from panel label
     - Federal Pacific Electric (FPE) Stab-Lok panels:
       * Active in FL homes built 1950s–1980s
       * CPSC study documented failure to trip under overcurrent conditions
         creating fire risk; estimated 2,800 fires per year nationally attributed
         to these panels
       * Must be reported as CRITICAL SAFETY DEFICIENCY
       * Insurance non-eligible with Citizens and most FL carriers
       * Required action: replace panel with listed equipment
     - Zinsco / Sylvania panels:
       * Manufactured 1950s–1970s; aluminum bus bars may allow breakers to
         weld to bus under fault conditions
       * Must be reported as CRITICAL SAFETY DEFICIENCY
       * Insurance non-eligible with most FL carriers
       * Required action: replace panel with listed equipment
     - Pushmatic (ITE/Bulldog): actuator mechanisms may fail; recommend
       evaluation by licensed electrician; not automatic insurance disqualifier
       but should be evaluated

  3. Wiring type:
     - Copper: standard; acceptable
     - Aluminum (branch circuits): critical deficiency per insurance guidelines
       * Found in homes built approximately 1965–1972 during copper shortage
       * Identified by silver-colored conductors labeled "AL" or "ALUM"
       * Connections to switches, outlets, and fixtures expand/contract
         differentially creating arcing and fire risk
       * Must be remediated with COPALUM crimp connectors (CPSC-approved
         permanent solution) or AlumiConn devices
       * Must be reported as requiring immediate evaluation and remediation
     - Knob-and-tube (K&T): pre-1950; open conductors on ceramic knobs
       and through porcelain tubes; no ground conductor
       * Non-insurable with most FL carriers without licensed evaluation
       * Cannot be covered with insulation per NEC 394.12(1)
       * Must be reported as critical deficiency for insurance purposes
     - Cloth-wrapped (NM cable with fabric jacket): found in homes built
       1930s–1960s; insulation becomes brittle with age; note presence
       and age-related risk

  4. GFCI protection presence:
     - Must verify in all required locations per NEC 210.8
     - Absence in any required location: report as safety deficiency
     - Test each GFCI device; non-responsive devices require replacement

  5. Electrical deficiency documentation for HO-800:
     - Form requires: "Yes/No" for problematic wiring types, panel type,
       GFCI presence, and any open grounds or unprotected circuits
     - Any "critical" item triggers re-inspection requirement before
       insurance can be issued or renewed

  Regulatory references for electrical findings:
     - NEC 2020 Art. 210.8 — GFCI Protection
     - NEC 2020 Art. 210.12 — AFCI Protection
     - NEC 2020 Art. 310.14 — Aluminum conductors
     - FBC E3901 — Receptacle Outlets
     - FBC E3902 — GFCI and AFCI Protection
     - Florida Statute 553.73 — Florida Building Code adoption (NEC incorporated by reference)
     - Citizens HO-800 Rev. 11/2021 Section 2
     - FL Statute 468.8319(b)

  Florida Statute 553.73 — Florida Building Code:
     The Florida Building Code is adopted pursuant to Florida Statute 553.73. The NEC 2020
     (National Electrical Code) is incorporated into the Florida Building Code and has the
     force of law throughout the State of Florida. All electrical installations must comply
     with NEC requirements as adopted via Florida Statute 553.73, including GFCI protection
     (NEC 210.8), AFCI protection (NEC 210.12), and conductor requirements (NEC 310.14).

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 3C — PLUMBING SYSTEM (Citizens HO-800 Section 3)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Inspector Responsibilities:
  The inspector must determine and document all of the following:

  1. Supply piping material:
     - Copper: preferred; highly durable; identify type L (standard residential)
       or type M (lighter wall, acceptable)
     - CPVC (cream/off-white chlorinated PVC): acceptable for hot and cold water
     - PEX (cross-linked polyethylene, red/blue/white flexible tubing): acceptable;
       modern construction standard; note manifold system if present
     - PVC: acceptable for cold water distribution only; not for hot water
     - Galvanized steel: identified by gray metallic pipe with threaded fittings;
       common in pre-1960s construction; flag if 30+ years old for corrosion risk
       and potential reduced flow; serviceable life typically 40–60 years
     - Polybutylene (PB): CRITICAL DEFICIENCY — see s. 468.8319(d) above
       * Gray or blue-gray flexible plastic pipe
       * Quest, Shell Oil, DuPont manufacture; marked "PB2110" on pipe
       * Compression fittings are gray acetal plastic or copper
       * Found in homes built approximately 1978–1995
       * Known to fail at fittings without warning due to chlorine degradation
         of acetal components; failure results in pinhole leaks or sudden splits
       * Citizens and most FL carriers will not insure property with PB without
         proof of complete replacement
       * Any visible PB must be reported as critical; inspector must inform
         client that PB may also be present in inaccessible areas

  2. Drain piping material:
     - PVC (white or gray): acceptable; modern standard
     - ABS (black): acceptable; common in older construction
     - Cast iron: durable; acceptable if not heavily corroded; serviceable
       life 50–75 years; inspect accessible sections for corrosion, pitting, or
       joint failures
     - Galvanized: older homes; flag if significant corrosion or restricted flow

  3. Water heater type, age, and condition:
     - Record all data as described in s. 468.8319(d)
     - TPR valve compliance is a Citizens HO-800 requirement
     - Non-compliant TPR discharge is a deficiency on the form

  4. Evidence of leaks:
     - Any active leak at supply, distribution, or drain connections must
       be noted as requiring immediate repair
     - Past leaks evidenced by water staining, mineral deposits, or soft
       flooring/cabinets must also be reported

  Regulatory references for plumbing findings:
     - FBC Plumbing Code P2903 — Water Supply System
     - FBC Plumbing Code P3002 — Drain, Waste, and Vent
     - FBC Plumbing Code P2803 — Temperature-Pressure Relief
     - Citizens HO-800 Rev. 11/2021 Section 3
     - FL Statute 468.8319(d)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 3D — HVAC SYSTEM (Citizens HO-800 Section 4)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Inspector Responsibilities:
  The inspector must determine and document all of the following:

  1. HVAC equipment type and age:
     - Record type: central split-system AC, heat pump, package unit,
       mini-split, window unit, electric strip heat, natural gas furnace
     - Record manufacturer, model number, and serial number; decode age
       from serial number format (varies by manufacturer)
     - HVAC equipment 15 years or older: flag as at or beyond expected
       serviceable life (typical life: central AC 12–15 years, heat pump 15 years)
     - Note if R-22 refrigerant system — critical flag for serviceability

  2. Operational status at time of inspection:
     - Document whether system was operated and results of operation
     - Note any failure to heat or cool, excessive noise, or operational anomalies
     - Document measured temperature differential (supply vs. return air)

  3. Ductwork condition:
     - Document duct type and insulation level in attic
     - Note any disconnected, crushed, collapsed, or significantly deteriorated
       duct sections
     - Disconnected ductwork in attic: major-to-critical finding depending on
       severity (conditioned air losses impact energy efficiency; pressurized
       attic creates moisture issues in humid Florida climate)

  4. Air filter condition:
     - Note condition of accessible air filter; heavily soiled filter may
       indicate maintenance neglect and potential coil damage

  5. Bathroom and kitchen ventilation:
     - All bathroom exhaust fans must vent to exterior; attic discharge is a
       code violation and moisture deficiency
     - Document compliance status

  Regulatory references for HVAC findings:
     - FBC Mechanical Code M1401 — General Requirements, HVAC Systems
     - FBC Mechanical Code M1501-M1502 — Exhaust Systems
     - FBC Energy Code R403 — Equipment and Systems
     - Citizens HO-800 Rev. 11/2021 Section 4
     - ASHRAE 62.2-2019 — Ventilation for Acceptable Indoor Air Quality
     - FL Statute 468.8319(c)

SECTION 4: WIND MITIGATION INSPECTION (OIR-B1-1802)
================================================================================

Wind Mitigation Inspections qualify homeowners for insurance premium discounts
under Florida Statute 627.0629 and the My Safe Florida Home program. The
inspection form is OIR-B1-1802 (Office of Insurance Regulation, Rev. 01/2012
and updates). Results may reduce homeowner's insurance premium by up to 60%.

References: FL Statute 627.0629; OIR-B1-1802 Form; FBC 6th/7th/8th Editions;
            Miami-Dade NOA Requirements; FL Product Approval Database.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 4A — BUILDING CODE (OIR-B1-1802 Item A)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Building Code determination based on original permit date:

  Pre-FBC (Built before March 1, 2002):
    - No wind credits available in most categories
    - Construction predates Florida Building Code requirements for wind
    - Inspector must document estimated year of construction from permit
      records, county property appraiser records, or structural evidence
    - Roofing permits after March 1, 2002 may qualify the roof covering
      for FBC credit even on pre-FBC structure

  FBC — Building Permit On or After March 1, 2002:
    - Eligible for credits based on construction features
    - Inspector must obtain permit number and document
    - New construction from 2002 onward was designed to FBC minimum standards

  Building Code Classification:
    - FBC 2001 (effective March 1, 2002 — December 31, 2004): first edition
    - FBC 2nd Edition (2004 — 2007)
    - FBC 3rd Edition (2007 — 2011)
    - FBC 4th Edition (2011 — 2014)
    - FBC 5th Edition (2014 — 2017)
    - FBC 6th Edition (2017 — 2020)
    - FBC 7th Edition (2020 — 2023)
    - FBC 8th Edition (effective January 1, 2024 — present)

  HVHZ Status:
    - High Velocity Hurricane Zone (HVHZ): Miami-Dade and Broward Counties
    - More stringent product approval and installation requirements
    - Non-HVHZ: All other Florida counties

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 4B — ROOF COVERING (OIR-B1-1802 Item B)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Credit available when roof covering was installed to current FBC standard:

  FBC Product Approval Requirement:
    - All roofing materials installed in Florida must carry FBC Product Approval
      (State of Florida approval number, format FL-XXXXX)
    - HVHZ requires Miami-Dade NOA (Notice of Acceptance) or FL Product Approval
    - Inspector must visually verify product approval label on installed materials
      when accessible (typically visible on shingle bundle labels, tile stacks,
      or membrane roll labels)
    - If product approval cannot be verified, no credit is available

  Credit Tiers:
    - No credit: roof covering does not meet FBC requirements; no product approval
    - Partial credit: FBC non-HVHZ standard
    - Maximum credit: FBC HVHZ standard (Miami-Dade or Broward) or verified
      product approval with compliance installation documentation

  Documentation for credit:
    - Inspector shall photograph approval labels when accessible
    - Permit documentation of roof installation supports credit
    - For tile: manufacturer ID plates on face of tiles where visible

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 4C — ROOF DECK ATTACHMENT (OIR-B1-1802 Item C)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  This is a significant credit factor. Inspector must access attic to determine:

  Nail type and spacing (inspect at minimum 8 locations distributed across roof):

  8d Common Nail (0.131" diameter × 2.5" length):
    - Industry standard for structural roof sheathing attachment
    - Provides highest uplift resistance
    - Required identification: round head with ring shank or smooth shank

  6d Common Nail (0.113" diameter × 2.0" length):
    - Lesser credit
    - Common in older construction

  Staples:
    - No wind mitigation credit
    - Prohibited by FBC for structural sheathing attachment (2002+)
    - Common in construction circa 1980s

  Nail Spacing:
    - Panels rated at 6/12: 6-inch spacing at panel edges, 12 inches in field
    - Panels rated at 6/6: 6-inch spacing throughout (maximum credit)
    - Panels rated at 6/6 equivalent or better qualify for maximum credit

  Maximum credit nail configurations:
    - 8d nails at 6/12 or better spacing: maximum credit
    - Ring-shank or screw-shank 8d nails with equivalent spacing: maximum credit

  Plywood thickness:
    - Minimum 19/32" (15/32" for pre-2002 construction)
    - OSB acceptable if meets APA structural rating and FBC approval

  Documentation:
    - Inspector must measure and document nail diameter, length, and spacing
    - Attic photos required for documentation
    - Where nail type cannot be determined, inspector must report as
      "undetermined" — no credit defaults apply

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 4D — ROOF-TO-WALL CONNECTIONS (OIR-B1-1802 Item D)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Roof-to-wall connection is the single most significant wind mitigation
  credit factor for most Florida homes. Connection type determines the
  extent to which roof structure can resist uplift forces during hurricanes.

  Connection Types (from lowest to highest credit):

  Toe-Nails Only:
    - 3 nails driven at an angle through rafter or truss bottom chord into
      top plate; no metal connector
    - Provides minimal uplift resistance
    - No wind mitigation credit; highest risk category
    - Common in Florida pre-1993 construction
    - Inspector must verify by visual inspection in attic at each rafter/truss

  Single Wrap (strap on one side of rafter):
    - Single metal strap wrapped around and attached to rafter and top plate
    - Moderate uplift resistance
    - Partial credit
    - Common in construction circa 1990s

  Double Wrap (straps on both sides):
    - Metal straps on both sides of rafter
    - Better uplift resistance than single wrap
    - Higher credit than single wrap

  Hurricane Clip (H-clip):
    - Single-sided metal connector designed specifically for rafter-to-plate
    - Provides specified uplift capacity based on fastener count and nail size
    - Moderate credit

  Structural / Engineered Connectors:
    - Simpson Strong-Tie H2.5A, H10, H14, HETA series
    - MiTek RT7, RT9 series
    - Any connector with published uplift capacity per ANSI/AF&PA NDS
    - Must visually confirm at minimum 90% of connections to claim credit
    - Maximum wind mitigation credit
    - Common in post-2002 FBC construction

  Documentation requirements:
    - Inspector must photograph representative connections in attic
    - Must identify connector manufacturer and model number where readable
    - Must estimate percentage of connections inspected and connector type
    - If mix of connection types, report the predominant (majority) type
    - Inaccessible attics: note as limitation; no credit without verification

  Impact on insurance premium:
    - Upgrade from toe-nail to engineered connector: potential 30–50% premium
      reduction depending on insurer; most significant single upgrade

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 4E — ROOF GEOMETRY (OIR-B1-1802 Item E)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Roof shape significantly affects wind resistance:

  Hip Roof (all roof planes slope down to eaves):
    - Most aerodynamic shape; resists wind from all directions
    - Maximum wind mitigation credit
    - Must be documented as >90% hip (minor gable dormers allowable)
    - Inspector must photograph roof geometry from multiple exterior angles

  Flat Roof (0:12 to 2:12 pitch):
    - Separate credit calculation; form addresses flat roofs separately
    - Modified bitumen and single-ply membranes appropriate covering
    - Inspector must verify ballast or mechanical fastening method

  Hip and Gable Combination:
    - Partial credit based on percentage of hip
    - Inspector must estimate and document percentage

  Gable or Other:
    - No geometry credit
    - Gable ends are structurally vulnerable to high-wind uplift
    - Gable end bracing: inspector may note presence of gable end bracing
      per FBC R602.10 and Miami-Dade requirements as a positive attribute

  Inspector must:
    - Photograph roof from all four elevations
    - Sketch roof geometry on form
    - Document predominant shape clearly

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 4F — OPENING PROTECTION (OIR-B1-1802 Item F)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Windows, doors, skylights, and garage doors must all be evaluated.
  Complete opening protection (all openings protected) provides maximum credit.

  Protection Levels (OIR-B1-1802 categories):

  A — No Protection:
    - Standard single-pane or double-pane windows without impact resistance
    - Standard entry doors without reinforcement
    - No credit

  B — Basic Protection (shutters or panels):
    - Accordion shutters, roll-down shutters, panel shutters
    - Must meet ASTM E1996 / E1886 Large Missile Impact standard
    - Must have FL Product Approval or Miami-Dade NOA
    - Inspector must verify product approval numbers on installed shutters

  C — Hurricane Impact Resistant Products (windows and doors):
    - Impact-resistant glazing (laminated glass with PVB interlayer or
      tempered glass meeting FBC impact requirements)
    - Reinforced frames meeting FBC wind load requirements
    - FL Product Approval or Miami-Dade NOA required
    - Inspector must locate and record product approval number from:
      * Label affixed to window/door frame
      * Etch mark in glass corner
      * Building permit documentation

  D — High-Velocity Hurricane Zone (HVHZ) Impact Resistant:
    - Miami-Dade or Broward county; highest wind design criteria
    - Maximum wind mitigation credit

  Garage Door Requirements:
    - Single-car garage door: rated for design wind pressure per FBC
    - Double-car (two-car wide) garage door: must meet FBC R609 and specific
      testing requirements; larger doors require heavier bracing
    - Minimum rating: 130 mph wind speed (inland); 150 mph wind speed (coastal)
    - Inspector must verify rating label on door header or manufacturer plate

  Documentation:
    - Inspector must record product approval number for every protected opening
    - Photograph labels and product stamps
    - If any opening lacks protection, opening protection level defaults to lowest
      present (all openings must be protected for maximum credit)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 4G — SECONDARY WATER RESISTANCE (OIR-B1-1802 Item G)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Secondary Water Resistance (SWR) credit available when self-adhering
  polymer modified bitumen underlayment (peel-and-stick membrane) is installed
  on the roof deck beneath the primary covering.

  Qualifying SWR products:
    - ASTM D1970 compliant self-adhering membrane
    - Common products: Grace Ice & Water Shield, Henry Blueskin SA, Polyglass
      Polystick, Carlisle WIP 300HT, or equivalent FL Product-Approved products
    - Must cover 100% of roof deck area for full credit
    - Partial coverage: no credit (form is binary)

  Inspector verification:
    - In attic: visible as dark (typically dark gray or black) rubberized
      membrane on the underside of roof decking boards
    - Must be distinguished from: standard felt/tar paper underlayment
      (lighter in color, less rubberized texture — does not qualify for SWR)
    - Alternative verification: roofing permit documentation showing specified
      self-adhering underlayment; contractor certificate

  Documentation:
    - Inspector must photograph attic underside of decking showing membrane
    - Note product name if visible from attic
    - If not accessible, mark as undetermined; no credit without verification

  SWR installation context:
    - Typically installed during re-roofing; homes with recent roof permits
      may have SWR even if original construction pre-dates requirement
    - FBC 6th Edition (2017) made SWR a standard requirement for re-roofing
      in many Florida counties; verify via permit records

SECTION 5: FLORIDA-SPECIFIC INSPECTION CONCERNS
================================================================================

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
5.1 — SINKHOLE INDICATORS AND REQUIRED REFERRALS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Florida's karst geology (soluble limestone bedrock) creates sinkhole risk
  statewide. Counties with highest risk: Hernando, Hillsborough, Pasco,
  Pinellas, Polk (the "Sinkhole Alley" region).

  Inspector must report and refer when observing:

  Exterior indicators:
    - Stair-step diagonal cracking in concrete block exterior walls at corners
    - Horizontal cracking in masonry foundation or stem walls
    - Separation of exterior walls from roof or floor structure
    - Visible circular or oval depression in ground adjacent to or under structure
    - Trees or fence posts leaning toward a central depression

  Interior indicators:
    - Diagonal cracking at door/window corners (45-degree stair-step pattern)
    - Floor slope exceeding 1 inch over 10 feet in any direction
    - Doors and windows that stick or will not close correctly
    - Separation of interior walls from ceiling
    - Cracks in tile flooring following grout lines in a pattern suggesting
      differential substrate movement

  Required inspector actions:
    - Note and photograph all observed indicators
    - Clearly communicate to client that sinkhole activity CANNOT be determined
      by visual home inspection
    - Recommend evaluation by a licensed geotechnical engineer
    - Do not use the word "sinkhole" in the report as a conclusion; use language
      such as: "differential foundation settlement consistent with possible
      sinkhole activity; refer to licensed geotechnical engineer for evaluation"
    - FL Statute 627.706 establishes sinkhole insurance requirements; insurer
      may require engineering report before issuing coverage on properties with
      sinkhole indicators

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
5.2 — CHINESE DRYWALL (2001–2009)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Certain Chinese-manufactured drywall installed in Florida homes built or
  remodeled between 2001 and 2009 emits hydrogen sulfide and other sulfur
  compounds causing:

  Observable indicators:
    - Blackened (corroded) copper conductors and coils, particularly:
      * Copper wire in electrical panel appearing black rather than orange/copper
      * HVAC evaporator coil fins blackened (normally silver/aluminum colored)
      * Refrigerant copper lines with black oxidation at fittings
    - Persistent sulfur/rotten egg odor, especially when HVAC is running
    - Pitting or blackening of exposed metal fasteners, plumbing, and fixtures

  Inspector actions:
    - Note all observable indicators
    - Advise client that definitive testing requires laboratory analysis of
      drywall samples
    - Refer for formal Chinese drywall testing if multiple indicators are present
    - Chinese drywall remediation typically requires complete replacement of
      drywall, electrical wiring, HVAC components, and all affected metals

  High-risk counties and construction periods:
    - Primary market: coastal SW Florida (Cape Coral, Fort Myers, Naples, Bonita
      Springs area), as well as Broward and Miami-Dade
    - Construction period: 2001–2009 (peak: 2005–2007 post-hurricane rebuilding)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
5.3 — WOOD-DESTROYING ORGANISMS (WDO)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  WDO inspection is SEPARATE from home inspection and requires a licensed
  pest control operator under FL Statute 482 and Florida Administrative
  Code 5E-14.

  Common WDO in Florida:
    - Eastern subterranean termites (Reticulitermes flavipes): most common;
      detected by mud tubes on foundation, sill plates, or piers
    - Formosan subterranean termites (Coptotermes formosanus): extremely
      destructive; large colonies; carton nests visible in walls/attic
    - Drywood termites (Incisitermes snyderi and others): detected by
      frass (fecal pellets) and hollow wood; require fumigation
    - Wood-boring beetles (powderpost, old house borer): detected by
      small round exit holes and frass; active infestations require treatment

  Inspector may note (but not diagnose):
    - Visible mud tubes on foundation walls, piers, or wood members
    - Hollow-sounding wood when tapped (attic framing, door frames, window sills)
    - Frass accumulations below wood members
    - Exit holes in wood surfaces
    - Damaged wood clearly consistent with insect activity

  Required inspector language:
    - "Evidence consistent with wood-destroying organism activity observed;
      evaluation and treatment recommendations by a licensed pest control
      operator under FL Statute 482 are recommended."
    - Inspector shall not identify specific WDO species or recommend specific
      treatments

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
5.4 — MOLD AND MOISTURE INTRUSION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Florida's humid subtropical climate makes moisture intrusion and mold
  among the most significant inspection concerns. Inspector must document:

  Visible indicators requiring notation:
    - Visible mold growth: appears as discoloration (black, green, gray, white)
      or fuzzy growth on surfaces; may have musty odor
    - Active water intrusion through roofing, windows, doors, or foundation
    - Moisture staining: brown or yellow staining on ceilings, walls, or floors
      indicating past or current water intrusion
    - Moisture meter readings >20% in wood substrates
    - Efflorescence (white mineral deposits) on masonry — indicates water
      migration through block or concrete

  High-risk areas in Florida homes:
    - Attic: improper ventilation or roof leaks cause condensation and mold
      on rafters, trusses, and decking
    - Behind exterior walls with improperly installed EIFS or synthetic stucco
    - Bathrooms with exhaust fans venting into attic
    - Crawlspaces (less common in FL but present in coastal Panhandle area)
    - Window and door frames with failed caulking or sealant

  Required inspector actions:
    - Note all visible mold growth with photographs
    - Measure and report moisture content of accessible wood substrates
    - Identify probable sources of moisture intrusion
    - Refer visible mold to licensed mold assessor/remediator under
      FL Statute 468 Part XVI for assessment and remediation scope
    - Inspector shall not:
      * Sample or test mold
      * Make health-related statements about mold species
      * Provide mold remediation recommendations (requires Part XVI license)

  Language guidance:
    - Appropriate: "Mold-like growth visible on attic decking; refer to
      licensed mold assessor for evaluation and remediation scope determination."
    - Inappropriate: "Toxic black mold present; health risk." (Species
      identification requires laboratory testing)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
5.5 — SEPTIC SYSTEMS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Septic system (onsite sewage treatment and disposal system — OSTDS) inspection
  requires a licensed contractor or engineer under FL Statute 381.0065 and
  FL Administrative Code 64E-6. A home inspector may NOT provide a septic
  system inspection report.

  Inspector may observe and note:
    - Evidence of drain field failure: saturated ground above drain field,
      sewage surfacing, sewage odor outdoors
    - Last pump-out date from homeowner disclosure or records
    - Location of septic tank and drain field relative to structure and well
    - Proximity of drain field to potable water well: minimum 75 feet required
      per FL Admin Code 64E-6.005
    - Proximity of drain field to surface water bodies: minimum 75 feet required
    - Unusual depressions or mounding above drain field area

  Required inspector language:
    - "Septic system is present. Septic system evaluation requires a licensed
      septic inspector; this inspection does not include the septic system.
      Evidence of [describe observation if applicable] was noted; licensed
      evaluation recommended."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
5.6 — WELL WATER SYSTEMS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Private well water systems require separate testing and inspection.
  Inspector may note:
    - Presence of pressure tank, pump, and visible components
    - Last water quality test date from homeowner disclosure
    - Evidence of pump cycling issues (short-cycling, unusual noise)
    - Well head location and condition (minimum 2 feet above grade, sealed cap)
    - Minimum well setbacks: 75 feet from septic drain field, 50 feet from
      septic tank per FL Admin Code 62-532

  Refer water quality testing to FL DOH or certified laboratory.

SECTION 6: FLORIDA BUILDING CODE — KEY RESIDENTIAL PROVISIONS
================================================================================

  Florida Statute 553.73 adopts the Florida Building Code; the 8th Edition
  (effective January 1, 2024) is the current edition. The FBC incorporates
  the International Residential Code (IRC) with Florida-specific amendments.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
FBC R401 — Foundation Requirements
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  R401.3 — Drainage: Final grade shall fall a minimum of 6 inches within the
  first 10 feet from the foundation perimeter to direct surface water away from
  the structure. Inspector must note reverse grading (grading toward foundation)
  as a deficiency contributing to moisture and potential foundation issues.

  R401.4 — Soil tests: Required when unusual soil conditions are suspected;
  geotechnical engineer referral required when sinkhole indicators are present.

  R403 — Footings: Footings shall be founded on undisturbed natural soils or
  engineered fill; minimum 12 inches below finished grade for non-frost areas
  (all of Florida meets this with much shallower frost considerations).

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
FBC R602 — Wood Wall Framing
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  R602.10 — Wall bracing: Specific requirements for wall bracing to resist
  lateral wind loads; Florida amendments require enhanced bracing consistent
  with wind exposure categories.

  Gable end bracing (FBC R602.10.11): Required in Exposure Category C and D
  (coastal Florida) to resist wind pressure on gable end triangular areas.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
FBC R905 — Roof Coverings
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  R905.1.1 — Roof decking: Solid or closely fitted decking required for
  certain products; maximum 2 layers of roofing material before complete tear-off.

  R905.2 — Asphalt shingles: Installation requirements per manufacturer and
  ASTM D3462 (glass-mat) or D225 (organic); minimum self-seal strip required.

  R905.3 — Clay and concrete tile: Minimum 2.5:12 slope for low-profile tile
  with underlayment per FBC; fastening requirements based on wind zone.

  R905.10 — Metal panel roofing: Must meet FBC Product Approval; installation
  per manufacturer and tested assembly.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
FBC R314 — Smoke Alarms
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  R314.3 — Locations:
  (a) In each sleeping room;
  (b) Outside each separate sleeping area in the immediate vicinity of the
      sleeping rooms;
  (c) On each additional story of the dwelling, including basements.

  R314.4 — Combination alarms: Combination smoke/carbon monoxide alarms
  acceptable where both are required.

  R314.6 — Alarm interconnection: In new construction, all alarms shall be
  interconnected so activation of one triggers all.

  Inspector must:
  - Test each smoke alarm using test button
  - Report any non-responsive or absent alarms as safety deficiencies
  - Report missing alarms in required locations

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
FBC R315 — Carbon Monoxide Alarms
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  R315.1 — Required in dwelling units with fuel-burning appliances or
  attached garages.

  R315.2 — Locations:
  (a) Outside each separate sleeping area in the immediate vicinity of the
      sleeping rooms;
  (b) On each level of the dwelling.

  Inspector must:
  - Verify presence in required locations
  - Test each CO alarm using test button
  - Report absent or non-responsive CO alarms as safety deficiencies

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
FBC R408 — Crawlspace Requirements
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Minimum 18 inches of clearance between finish grade and bottom of floor
  framing in crawlspace. Vapor retarder (6-mil polyethylene minimum) required
  on ground surface. Ventilation: 1 square foot of vent area per 150 square
  feet of crawlspace floor area unless ground is covered with Class I vapor
  retarder (then 1:1500 ratio allowed).

SECTION 7: NATIONAL ELECTRICAL CODE (NEC 2020) — FLORIDA PROVISIONS
================================================================================

  The NEC 2020 is adopted in Florida via the Florida Building Code, 7th and
  8th Editions. Florida adopts NEC with specific state amendments.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
NEC Art. 210.8 — Ground-Fault Circuit-Interrupter Protection
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  GFCI protection required for all 125-volt and 250-volt receptacles in:

  (A) Dwelling Units:
    (1) Bathrooms — all receptacles in bathrooms
    (2) Garages — all receptacles in garages including attached and detached
    (3) Outdoors — all receptacles at grade level and above grade level
    (4) Crawlspaces — all receptacles at or below grade level
    (5) Unfinished basements — all receptacles
    (6) Kitchen — all receptacles within 6 feet of a sink
    (7) Laundry areas — all receptacles within 6 feet of a sink
    (8) Boathouses — all receptacles
    (9) Bathtub or shower areas — all receptacles within 6 feet
    (10) Indoor and outdoor swimming pools, spas, and hot tubs — all receptacles
         within 20 feet
    (11) Sinks located in areas other than kitchens — within 6 feet
    (12) Garages and accessory buildings — all receptacles

  Inspector must test all GFCI devices using a GFCI outlet tester to verify
  proper operation. Non-responsive GFCI outlets require replacement. Reporting
  absent GFCI protection in any required location as a safety deficiency is
  required under FAC 61-30 and FL Statute 468.8319(b).

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
NEC Art. 210.12 — Arc-Fault Circuit-Interrupter Protection (AFCI)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  AFCI protection required for all 120-volt, 15A and 20A branch circuits
  supplying outlets in:
  - Kitchens
  - Family rooms
  - Dining rooms
  - Living rooms
  - Parlors
  - Libraries
  - Dens
  - Bedrooms
  - Sunrooms
  - Recreation rooms
  - Closets
  - Hallways
  - Laundry areas
  - Similar rooms and areas in dwelling units

  FBC E3902.12 adopts this requirement. Combination-type AFCI breakers required
  for branch circuit protection.

  Inspector note: AFCI requirements apply to new construction and substantial
  renovations; absence of AFCI in older homes is not a code violation for
  existing installations, but should be noted as a safety upgrade recommendation.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
NEC Art. 310.14 — Aluminum Conductors
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  (A) Solid aluminum conductors:
    - 8 AWG or larger aluminum conductors may be used for service entrance and
      feeder applications
    - Solid aluminum conductors 10 AWG or smaller are prohibited in branch
      circuits installed after applicable code cycle

  (B) Aluminum branch circuit wiring (15A and 20A):
    - Identified by silver-colored conductors; wire may be labeled "AL" or "ALUM"
    - Devices and fixtures must be rated "CO/ALR" (Copper/Aluminum Revised) for
      connection to aluminum wiring
    - CPSC recommends COPALUM crimp connectors (permanent solution) or AlumiConn
      connectors (approved alternative) for each connection point
    - Inspector must report aluminum branch circuit wiring as requiring
      evaluation and remediation; include insurance impact notation

SECTION 8: DBPR DISCIPLINARY ACTIONS AND ENFORCEMENT
================================================================================

  The Florida Department of Business and Professional Regulation (DBPR),
  Division of Professions, enforces Chapter 468 Part XV. The Board of
  Home Inspectors does not exist as a separate board; the department itself
  administers the program.

  Complaint Process:
    (1) Any person may file a complaint with DBPR against a licensed inspector
    (2) DBPR investigates; probable cause determination by probable cause panel
    (3) If probable cause found: Administrative complaint filed
    (4) Proceedings under Chapter 120 (APA), Chapter 455 (DBPR disciplinary)
    (5) Settlement or formal hearing; penalties as in s. 468.8324

  Common violations resulting in discipline:
    - Failing to inspect required systems without proper notation
    - Misrepresenting findings in a written report
    - Performing work on inspected properties (conflict of interest)
    - Allowing unlicensed persons to perform or sign inspections
    - Failing to provide required disclosures before inspection
    - Accepting undisclosed compensation from parties with interest
      in the transaction

  Statute of limitations:
    - Complaint must be filed within 2 years of the date the inspector
      knew or should have known of the alleged violation
    - 6-year absolute bar from date of alleged violation

SECTION 10: AI-GENERATED REPORT DISCLAIMER REQUIREMENTS
================================================================================

  Per Florida Statute 468.8314, 468.8323(5), and 468.8326(3), any home
  inspection report containing AI-generated or automated narrative content
  must include a disclosure in each AI-generated section. The required
  disclosure language is:

  "This narrative was AI-generated and requires review and approval by the
  licensed inspector of record per Florida Statute 468.8314."

  This disclaimer:
  (a) Informs the client that narrative content was generated by an AI system;
  (b) Identifies the applicable Florida statute governing inspector responsibility;
  (c) Makes explicit that the licensed inspector bears responsibility for the
      content regardless of how it was generated;
  (d) Is required in each AI-generated section, not merely once in the report.

  Failure to include this disclaimer in an AI-assisted inspection report
  constitutes a violation of s. 468.8323(5) and may result in disciplinary
  action under s. 468.8324.

  The AuditAgent verifies the presence of this disclaimer in each narrative
  section as part of the quality gate process before report delivery.
