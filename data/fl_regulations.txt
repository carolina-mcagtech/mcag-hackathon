SECTION 1: FLORIDA STATUTE 468 PART XV — HOME INSPECTORS LICENSING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

468.8311 — Definitions
  "Home inspection" means a limited, non-invasive, visual examination of the
  readily accessible areas of a residential building, performed for a fee,
  designed to identify defects within specific systems and components of a
  building that are both observed and deemed material by the home inspector.

  "Home inspector" means a person who is licensed under this part to perform
  home inspections.

  "Inspector of record" means the licensed home inspector who performs the
  inspection and signs the home inspection report.

468.8314 — Licensure required
  A person may not perform, or offer to perform, a home inspection for
  compensation unless that person holds a valid home inspector license issued
  by the Department of Business and Professional Regulation (DBPR).

468.8319 — Minimum inspection requirements
  A licensed home inspector must inspect all of the following systems when
  present and accessible:
  (a) Structural system — foundation, grading, roof covering, roof structure,
      walls, columns, floors, ceilings, attic, and framing.
  (b) Electrical system — service entrance, panels, branch circuits, connected
      devices and fixtures, smoke detectors, and carbon monoxide detectors.
  (c) HVAC system — heating and cooling equipment, ductwork distribution,
      thermostats, and energy sources.
  (d) Plumbing system — interior water supply and distribution, drains, waste
      and vents, water heaters, and all fixtures and faucets.
  (e) Appliances — installed dishwashers, ranges, ovens, garbage disposals,
      microwave ovens, bathroom exhaust fans.

468.8323 — Standards of practice
  Home inspectors shall follow the Standards of Practice adopted by rule by
  the Florida Building Commission. Reports shall identify:
  (a) The systems and components inspected.
  (b) Deficiencies in the condition of inspected systems or components.
  (c) Which systems or components were not inspected and the reason.

468.8326 — Report requirements
  The written inspection report must:
  (a) Describe the condition of inspected systems clearly and without
      ambiguity.
  (b) Include photographs of material defects.
  (c) Be delivered to the client within three business days of the inspection.
  (d) Identify the inspector of record and license number.
  (e) State the limitations of the inspection.

SECTION 2: 4-POINT INSPECTION REQUIREMENTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The 4-Point Inspection evaluates only four major systems for insurance
underwriting purposes, particularly for older homes (generally 30+ years).
Florida insurers widely require this form, which follows the Citizens Property
Insurance Corporation format.

--- 2A. ROOF SYSTEM ---

Roof Age & Condition:
  - Inspector must determine and report the estimated age of the roof covering.
  - Roof covering material must be identified: asphalt shingle, tile (concrete
    or clay), metal (standing seam or exposed fastener), built-up/modified
    bitumen, or other.
  - Condition rating: Excellent / Good / Fair / Poor.
  - Any evidence of leaks, missing shingles, ponding water, improper
    flashings, or inadequate drainage must be documented.
  - Remaining useful life estimate required (in years).

Deficiency triggers requiring immediate notation:
  * Active roof leaks or water stains at ceiling/decking indicating prior leak.
  * Shingles showing granule loss exceeding 25% of surface area.
  * Fascia or soffit damage, missing or deteriorated flashing.
  * Evidence of prior repairs without permit (visible re-nailing patterns,
    mismatched materials).
  * Flat roofs with standing water or blistering membrane.
  * Roof decking visible sagging or structural deformation.

Regulatory reference: FBC R905 — Requirements for Roof Coverings.
  FBC R906 — Roof Insulation. Citizens 4-Point Form HO-800 Rev. 11/2021.

--- 2B. ELECTRICAL SYSTEM ---

Panel & Service:
  - Service amperage must be determined: 60A, 100A, 150A, 200A, or greater.
  - Panel manufacturer and type must be identified.
  - Inspector must flag any Federal Pacific (Stab-Lok), Zinsco/Sylvania,
    or pushmatic panels as high-risk and insurance-ineligible in most cases.
  - Double-tapped breakers must be noted (two conductors on a single breaker
    not designed for it).
  - Breaker condition: any evidence of heat damage, corrosion, or tripping.

Wiring:
  - Aluminum wiring in branch circuits (post-1965) must be identified and
    flagged — requires COPALUM crimp connectors or AlumiConn devices per
    NEC 310.14 and Florida requirements.
  - Knob-and-tube wiring (pre-1950) must be identified and noted as
    non-insurable by most FL carriers.
  - Cloth-wrapped wiring must be documented.
  - GFCI protection: required within 6 feet of any water source (NEC 210.8),
    in garages, unfinished basements, crawlspaces, and exterior locations.
  - AFCI protection: required in all habitable rooms per FBC E3902.

Deficiency triggers:
  * Exposed or unprotected wiring.
  * Open junction boxes or missing cover plates.
  * Evidence of DIY wiring (improper splices, missing wire nuts).
  * Missing breaker knockouts.
  * Main disconnect not labeled or inaccessible.

Regulatory reference: NEC 2020 (adopted in Florida via FBC 7th/8th Edition).
  Florida Statute 553.73 — Florida Building Code adoption.

--- 2C. PLUMBING SYSTEM ---

Supply Lines:
  - Material must be identified: copper, CPVC, PVC, PEX, galvanized, polybutylene.
  - Polybutylene pipe (gray plastic, commonly installed 1978–1995) is a
    critical deficiency — it has a known failure history and is non-insurable
    with most FL carriers. Manufacturer identifiers: Quest, Shell Oil, DuPont.
  - Galvanized pipe older than 40 years must be flagged for corrosion risk.

Drain, Waste & Vent:
  - Material: cast iron, ABS, PVC, or galvanized.
  - Cast iron showing significant corrosion or pitting must be noted.

Water Heater:
  - Age, fuel type, capacity (gallons), and condition must be documented.
  - Water heaters 15+ years old are flagged as at end of serviceable life.
  - Temperature-pressure relief (TPR) valve must be present and properly
    discharged to exterior or floor drain; improper discharge (e.g., capped
    or discharged overhead) is a code violation.
  - No water heaters installed in sleeping rooms or closets opening to them
    (FBC R2004.1).

Deficiency triggers:
  * Active leaks at any supply or drain connection.
  * Polybutylene supply piping.
  * Galvanized drain lines with visible corrosion or blockage.
  * Water heater without TPR valve or with improper discharge.
  * Evidence of sewage odor (possible venting failure).

Regulatory reference: FBC Plumbing Code Chapter 6 (Water Supply & Distribution).
  FBC Plumbing Code Chapter 7 (Sanitary Drainage).

--- 2D. HVAC SYSTEM ---

Equipment:
  - Age, type (central air/heat, heat pump, mini-split, window unit), and
    condition of all heating and cooling equipment must be documented.
  - HVAC equipment 15+ years old flagged as approaching end of serviceable life.
  - Refrigerant type must be noted; R-22 systems are being phased out and
    parts/refrigerant availability is limited.

Ductwork:
  - Material (sheet metal, flex duct, ductboard) and insulation must be noted.
  - Disconnected, crushed, or uninsulated ductwork in unconditioned spaces
    must be flagged.
  - Ductwork in attics must be insulated to minimum R-6 per FBC Energy Code.

Ventilation:
  - Bathroom exhaust fans must vent to exterior (not attic).
  - Dryer vents must terminate at building exterior.

Deficiency triggers:
  * Refrigerant leaks (oil staining at fittings, ice formation on lines).
  * Missing or severely clogged air filters.
  * Inoperable heating or cooling during inspection.
  * Disconnected duct runs.
  * Ventilation terminating into attic space.

Regulatory reference: FBC Mechanical Code Chapter 13 (Commercial) / Chapter 14.
  ASHRAE 62.2 — Ventilation for Acceptable Indoor Air Quality.

SECTION 3: WIND MITIGATION INSPECTION CRITERIA
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Wind Mitigation Inspections are conducted to qualify homeowners for insurance
premium discounts under Florida Statute 627.0629. The inspection is documented
on the OIR-B1-1802 form (Office of Insurance Regulation).

--- 3A. BUILDING CODE ---

  Inspector must determine the building permit date to classify:
  - Pre-FBC: Built before March 1, 2002 (no wind credits in most categories).
  - FBC 2001 or later: Eligible for credits based on construction features.
  - Post-2001 construction: Document permit number and year.

--- 3B. ROOF COVERING ---

  Roof covering must meet FBC High-Velocity Hurricane Zone (HVHZ) or
  non-HVHZ standards based on county:
  - HVHZ counties: Miami-Dade, Broward.
  - Non-HVHZ: All other Florida counties.

  Credits available for:
  * FBC product approval number present on installed materials.
  * Roof covering installed to current FBC standard.

--- 3C. ROOF DECK ATTACHMENT ---

  Inspector must determine nail type and spacing by accessing attic:
  - 8d nails (0.131" x 2.5") at 6"/12" spacing: Maximum credit.
  - 8d nails at 6"/6" spacing: Maximum credit (HVHZ equivalent).
  - 6d nails or clips: Lesser or no credit.
  - Staples: No credit.

  Plywood thickness determines structural adequacy:
  - Minimum 19/32" (15/32" pre-2002 construction).

--- 3D. ROOF-TO-WALL CONNECTIONS ---

  This is one of the most significant rating factors. Inspector must visually
  identify connector type via attic access:

  a) Single wraps (strap on one side of rafter): Minimal credit.
  b) Double wraps (straps on both sides of rafter): Moderate credit.
  c) Clips (hurricane clip, H-clip): Moderate credit.
  d) Single/double wraps with embedded connection: Higher credit.
  e) Structural/engineered connectors (Simpson Strong-Tie H10, H14, etc.):
     Maximum credit — must visually confirm at minimum 90% of connections.
  f) Toe nails only (3 nails per rafter): No credit — highest risk category.

  Common Florida connectors:
  - Simpson Strong-Tie H2.5, H10, H2.5A, HETA: provide rated uplift capacity.
  - MiTek RT7, RT9 series: equivalent engineering-rated connections.

--- 3E. ROOF GEOMETRY ---

  Hip roof (all sides slope to eaves): Maximum wind credit — most aerodynamic.
  Hip roof must be documented as >90% hip for credit.
  Flat/Low slope (<2:12 pitch): Separate credit calculation.
  Gable/Other: No geometry credit.

  Inspector must sketch or photograph roof geometry from exterior.

--- 3F. OPENING PROTECTION ---

  Windows, doors, skylights, and garage doors must be evaluated:

  a) No protection: No credit.
  b) Basic: Panels meeting ASTM E1996 Large Missile Impact.
  c) Hurricane Impact Resistant (FBC Product Approval required):
     - Windows/doors with impact glass and reinforced frames.
     - Miami-Dade NOA (Notice of Acceptance) or FL Product Approval Number.
  d) High-Velocity Hurricane Zone rated: Maximum credit.

  Inspector must verify:
  - Product approval number on window/door frame or label.
  - Absence of gaps or broken seals in impact products.
  - Garage door rating: minimum rated for 130 mph (inland) or 150 mph (coastal).

--- 3G. SECONDARY WATER RESISTANCE (SWR) ---

  Peel-and-stick self-adhering underlayment (e.g., Grace Ice & Water Shield,
  Henry Blueskin) applied to roof deck under shingles provides SWR credit.
  Inspector must verify in attic — visible as dark rubberized membrane on
  underside of decking, or via photos during re-roofing documented permits.

  Products qualifying for SWR:
  - ASTM D1970 compliant self-adhering polymer modified bitumen sheet.
  - Must cover 100% of deck area for full credit.

SECTION 4: COMMON FLORIDA-SPECIFIC INSPECTION CONCERNS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

4.1 — SINKHOLE INDICATORS
  Florida's karst geology creates sinkhole risk statewide, particularly in:
  Hernando, Hillsborough, Pasco, Pinellas, Polk counties.
  Inspection flags:
  - Diagonal cracking at door/window corners (stair-step pattern in block).
  - Foundation settlement or uneven floors (>1" slope across 10 feet).
  - Circular depression in yard, especially near structure.
  Note: Home inspectors are NOT licensed to determine sinkhole activity;
  findings must be referred to a licensed geotechnical engineer.

4.2 — CHINESE DRYWALL (2001–2009 era)
  Imported Chinese drywall emits sulfur compounds causing:
  - Corroded copper wiring (blackened conductors and coils).
  - Corroded HVAC evaporator coils and refrigerant lines.
  - Persistent sulfur/rotten egg odor.
  Primarily in homes built or remodeled 2001–2009. Must be referred for
  formal testing if suspected.

4.3 — WOOD-DESTROYING ORGANISMS (WDO)
  WDO inspection is SEPARATE from home inspection and requires a licensed
  pest control operator (FL Statute 482). Common in Florida:
  - Subterranean termites (Reticulitermes and Coptotermes formosanus — Formosan).
  - Drywood termites (require fumigation).
  - Wood-boring beetles.
  Inspector may note visible evidence (mud tubes, frass, hollow wood) but must
  refer to licensed WDO inspector for treatment recommendations.

4.4 — MOLD & MOISTURE
  Florida's humid subtropical climate makes moisture intrusion a primary concern.
  Inspector must document:
  - Visible mold growth (appears as discoloration, fuzzy growth).
  - Moisture readings >20% in wood substrates.
  - Staining at ceiling, walls, or floor indicating water intrusion.
  - Improper slope/drainage toward foundation.
  Mold remediation must be referred to a licensed mold remediator (FL Statute 468 Part XVI).

4.5 — SEPTIC SYSTEMS
  Septic system inspection is separate from home inspection; requires a
  licensed septic inspector. Inspector may note:
  - Visible drain field failure (soggy ground, odor, surfacing effluent).
  - Last pump-out date from homeowner disclosure.
  - Proximity of drain field to well (minimum 75 feet per FL Statute 381).
